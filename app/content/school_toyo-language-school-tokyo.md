## üìö Courses, Curriculum & Teaching Style

Toyo Language School primarily offers the *Shingaku Katei* (Academic Advancement Course), designed explicitly to transition students from foundational Japanese skills to the necessary academic level required for matriculation into Japanese higher education. The school accommodates various student timelines through multiple entry points: the standard 2-year course starting in April, 1.9-year starting in July, 1.6-year starting in October, and 1.3-year starting in January, alongside specialized 1-year courses for highly advanced entrants or specific short-term goals. This flexible intake system ensures that students can start their Japanese journey almost year-round, aligning with global academic calendars, while all courses maintain the same intense preparation focus.

Upon entry, students are rigorously placed into classes based on comprehensive placement testing that assesses grammar, listening, reading, writing, and speaking ability. The curriculum is meticulously segmented, moving systematically from beginner levels (N5/N4 equivalent) through to advanced academic Japanese (N1 equivalent and beyond). The teaching methodology is highly structured, emphasizing balanced skill development necessary for academic success. Initial levels focus heavily on foundational grammar acquisition, vocabulary expansion, and structural integrity of the language. As students progress into intermediate and advanced levels, the curriculum shifts dramatically toward application, critical thinking, and advanced linguistic tasks.

Key components of the curriculum include intensive preparation for standardized exams. Dedicated class hours are allocated specifically to EJU preparation, covering not only the Japanese section (which requires high-level reading comprehension and structural understanding) but also the auxiliary subjects demanded by universities, such as Mathematics, Science (Physics, Chemistry, Biology), and Japan and the World (Social Studies). These EJU subject classes are often taught by native Japanese instructors specialized in those specific academic disciplines, a crucial feature that many smaller schools cannot replicate. This integrated approach ensures that students are not merely learning general Japanese, but the precise academic vocabulary and expression needed to study subjects like Engineering or Economics in Japanese.

Furthermore, the school provides specialized tracks that cater to niche student needs. The dedicated Art and Design Pathway Program is particularly noteworthy. This track provides intensive Japanese language training while integrating portfolio development assistance, preparation for art school entrance exams (which often involve drawing practicals or specialized interviews), and critical exposure to art history and terminology in Japanese. This specific specialization is vital for students targeting institutions renowned for art and design, such as Musashino Art University, Tama Art University, or Tokyo University of the Arts, requiring a tailored approach beyond general academic language. The teaching style across all tracks involves extensive use of mock exams, timed essay writing exercises, formal presentation practice, and sophisticated critical reading assignments using authentic Japanese academic texts to simulate university-level work.
