Navigating the job market in Japan requires mastering a highly standardized and culturally unique application process. At the heart of this process is the **Rirekisho (履歴書)**—the Japanese resume. Whether you are applying for a part-time job (*baito*), a post-graduation career path via *Shukatsu* (job hunting), or a mid-career transition, submitting a flawless Rirekisho is your first and most critical hurdle.

In the modern Japanese hiring landscape, while digital submissions (via PDF or specialized job portals) have become the default standard, the structural rules, linguistic nuances, and strict formatting conventions remain unchanged. This comprehensive guide walks you through every section of the Japanese resume, provides downloadable templates, and highlights the subtle mistakes that can make or break your application.

## 1. Rirekisho vs. Shokumu-keirekisho: Understanding the Difference

Before filling out any forms, you must understand the distinction between Japan’s two main application documents: the **Rirekisho (履歴書)** and the **Shokumu-keirekisho (職務経歴書)**. Failing to provide both when requested, or confusing their purposes, is a common reason international applicants are filtered out early.

| Feature | Rirekisho (履歴書) | Shokumu-keirekisho (職務経歴書) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Purpose** | Personal profile, basic bio-data, education, and chronological overview of your career. | Detailed breakdown of professional achievements, projects, skills, and work methodologies. |
| **Typical Length** | Strictly 1 to 2 pages (standardized templates). | 1 to 3 pages (free format allowed, though structured). |
| **Target Audience** | Required for all levels: part-time (*baito*), fresh graduates, and mid-career professionals. | Primarily required for mid-career professionals and specialized corporate roles. |
| **Key Focus Areas** | Certifications, academic milestones, basic contact details, and a brief motivation statement. | Specific business accomplishments, technical stack, team management experience, and quantifiable KPIs. |

If you are a student applying for a part-time job, a Rirekisho is usually all you need. However, for full-time career positions, recruiters expect both documents to be submitted together.

## 3. Step-by-Step Writing Guide for the Rirekisho

Every section of the Rirekisho has strict formatting expectations. Here is how to write each field without making critical cultural errors.

A standard rirekisho usually includes, in order:

1. **Basic information** (name, contact) and a photo that follows strict rules
2. **Academic & work history** (chronological)
3. **Licenses & certifications** (JLPT, technical, etc.)
4. **Motivation & self-PR** / personal statement
5. **Commuting info & special requests**

### Basic Information (基本情報)
This header section contains your core contact details and identity.

*   **Date (日付):** Write the date you are **submitting** the resume, not the day you wrote it. If you are mailing it, use the postmark date. If you are emailing it or uploading it to a portal, use the submission date. Consistently use either Western years (*Seireki*, e.g., 2026) or Japanese imperial years (*Wareki*, e.g., Reiwa 8) throughout the entire document. Do not mix them.
*   **Name (氏名):** Write your full name as it appears on your passport or Residence Card. For international names, write your name in English characters, but always include the reading assistance (*Furigana*) directly above it.
    *   *Tip:* If the template says **ふりがな** (hiragana), write your name’s reading in hiragana. If it says **フリガナ** (katakana), write it in katakana.
*   **Birth Date & Age (生年月日・満年齢):** Provide your date of birth. The "満" age is your actual age on the day of submission.
*   **Address (住所):** Do not skip prefectures or apartment building names. Write your address out completely (e.g., Tokyo-to, Shinjuku-ku... followed by the building name and room number). Provide the Furigana for your address up to the block number (building names do not need Furigana unless highly unusual).
*   **Contact Information (連絡先):** Provide a reliable Japanese phone number and a professional email address (avoid unprofessional handles).

### Academic and Work History (学歴・職歴)
This section is divided into two parts but written in one continuous table block. 

#### Rules for Academic History (学歴)
1.  Write **学歴** centered on the first line of the table.
2.  List your education chronologically from oldest to newest.
3.  For high school, you only need to write the graduation date (e.g., "〇〇 High School Graduation").
4.  For higher education (Language school, Vocational school, University), write both the entrance (**入学**) and graduation/expected graduation (**卒業** or **卒業見込み**) on separate lines.
5.  Include your specific department, major, and faculty details. Do not abbreviate school names.

#### Rules for Work History (職歴)
1.  Leave one line blank after your academic history, then write **職歴** centered on the next line.
2.  List your employment history chronologically.
3.  Write the official name of the company, the department, your role, and the status of employment (e.g., Full-time: 正社員, Part-time: アルバイト).
4.  Write entrance (**入社**) and resignation (**退社**) on separate lines. If you are still working, write **現在に至る** (Currently employed here) on the line below your last entry, aligned to the left, and write **以上** (End) aligned to the far right on the final line.


**Example academic / work history layout** (year → month → entry):

| Year | Month | Academic / Work History |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| 2018 | 4 | 〇〇大学 人文学部 国際文化学科 入学 |
| 2022 | 3 | 〇〇大学 人文学部 国際文化学科 卒業 |
| | | **職歴** |
| 2022 | 4 | 株式会社アークシステムズ 入社 (正社員) |
| | | システム開発部にてエンジニアとして勤務 |
| 2025 | 10 | 株式会社アークシステムズ 一身上の都合により退社 |
| | | 現在に至る |
| | | 以上 |

### Licenses and Certifications (免許・資格)
This is where you showcase your language proficiency, technical skills, and industry qualifications.
*   List certifications chronologically.
*   Write out the full, official name of each certification. For example, instead of writing "JLPT N1", write **日本語能力試験N1 合格** (Japanese Language Proficiency Test N1 Passed).
*   If you are currently studying for a relevant license or test, write the target date and state **取得に向けて勉強中** (Currently studying to acquire).
*   If you have a driver’s license, list it as **普通自動車第一種運転免許 取得**.

### Motivation and Self-PR (志望動機・自己PR)
This is your primary opportunity to pitch your value. Avoid generic phrases and structure your response using the following framework:

1.  **The Hook (Why this company):** Clearly state what attracted you to this specific organization. Mention their values, recent business achievements, or market positioning.
2.  **Your Experience (What you bring):** Directly connect your academic background or past work experience to the role.
3.  **Future Contribution (The value add):** Explain how you plan to utilize your skills to solve their specific challenges.

*Tip for students/baito:* Keep this section concise (around 150–200 words if digital). Ensure it reads naturally in professional Japanese (*Keigo*).

## 4. The Critical Rules of the Rirekisho Photo

In Japan, the photo on your resume is subject to extreme scrutiny. It is often the very first element a hiring manager looks at, and a poor-quality photo can signal a lack of seriousness or cultural awareness.

### Physical Specifications & Appearance Standards
*   **Dimensions:** Exactly 4.0 cm high by 3.0 cm wide.
*   **Recency:** Taken within the last 3 months.
*   **Background:** Solid blue, white, or light gray. No shadows or distracting elements.
*   **Dress Code:** Professional business attire. For both men and women, this means a dark suit jacket (black, navy, or charcoal), a crisp white collared shirt, and a clean-cut, conservative hairstyle.
*   **Expression:** A gentle smile with closed lips. Ensure your eyes are fully visible (pushed-back bangs) and look directly into the camera.

### Digital Submissions
If submitting your resume digitally:
*   Do not crop a casual selfie. Go to a professional photo studio or use a high-quality "Ki-Re-i" photo booth found at major train stations in Japan, which provides a digital download option via a QR code.
*   The photo file should be high-resolution, perfectly cropped, and centered over the designated photo box on your digital template.

## 5. Formatting, Handwriting, and Submission Etiquette

While content is vital, presentation mistakes can disqualify you instantly. Keep these strict rules in mind:

### The Handwriting vs. Typing Debate
In the past, traditional Japanese companies preferred hand-written Rirekisho using black ballpoint pens because penmanship was believed to reflect character. However, this has shifted significantly.
*   **IT, global companies, and start-ups:** Strongly prefer typed, digital copies (Excel/PDF).
*   **Traditional industries, manufacturing, and local hospitality:** May still value or require hand-written submissions.
*   *Rule of thumb:* If the application details do not specify, a clean, digitally typed PDF is highly professional and widely accepted.

### Date Formats: consistency is Key
You must choose one dating system and stick with it throughout your entire application packet (including your cover letter and Shokumu-keirekisho):
*   **Seireki (西暦):** Western years (e.g., 2026).
*   **Wareki (和暦):** Japanese Imperial years (e.g., Reiwa 8 / 令和8年).
*   If you use *Wareki*, never abbreviate years (do not write "R8", write "令和8年").

### Prohibited Actions
*   **Never use erasable pens (like Frixion pens).** If submitting a handwritten copy, use a high-quality black gel or ballpoint pen.
*   **Never use correction fluid (white-out).** If you make a mistake on a hand-written resume, you must discard the sheet and start over.
*   **Do not leave empty spaces.** If you have no licenses, write **なし** (None) rather than leaving the field blank.
